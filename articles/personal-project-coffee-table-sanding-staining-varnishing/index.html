Personal Project: Coffee Table - Sanding, Staining, Varnishing

Fri 21 01 2011

[![image][]][image]

Over the recent months I have taken on a number of projects to improve the look this house. In this case, I found a small wooden coffee table at a Value Village for $19.99 and I saw potential in it. I could visualize the look of it finished, even as it sit in the furniture section of Value Village. The table as I bought it was not stained at all - it had a number of large gashes on the top of it and the varnish had a somewhat off-color to it. The great thing about this table (besides the price) was how sturdy it appeared to be built. Nothing screams "project" like some basic solid wood. I'm going to show you how to take a coffee table, stain it and make it look great. This can be done for easily less than $30 ($70, if you can't borrow a sander).

[][] Coffee Table: The original look of the table

Sanding is usually the most important part of the project. You want to make sure you get the previous varnish off the wood and yet don't want to take too much of the wood with it. To start with, I used an orbital sander with the (Velcro sanding sheets). These are generally about $40 at a hardware store. I started with 60 grit sandpaper. As I was sanding I decided I wanted to stain as much of the table as possible - so I took it apart into pieces. The roof of my truck was quite useful for doing some of this work from waist-level. Here's a look a piece of the table and the sander I was using:

[![image][2]][]

After sanding everything with the 60 grit sandpaper, I moved on to the 120 grit sandpaper to smooth out the rough surfaces. Usually just two passes of sandpaper is enough, even though some will tell you that you need to do a third (possibly with 180 grit). With this finished, I was happy with the sanding stage.

Sanding Tip: Sand in the direction of the grain of the wood. Also, I was doing all this outside of my garage to keep from getting dust all over - when sanding, be sure to wear a mask!

[][] The small bottle of stain used

After the sanding was finished, I started to stain. I used Varathane Professional Premium Wood Stain - American Walnut (236ml) - for this job. As you can see by the size of the can, very little stain is required. This can was half-empty when I started and still has some stain left - you will not use much of this stuff. I used a natural paintbrush and simply started to stain (continuing to stay with the grain).

Staining Tip: It's sloppy and tends to really show later if you make mistakes. I only placed one coat of the stain on the wood - but after the first coat I took the time to touch up some of the spots that I missed on the wood. be consistent and even whenever possible.

After letting the stain dry for a week ( yea, I gave it extra time), I came back to start the lacquer stage. The varnish is used to protect the wood and stain and create the finish of the table. For this process I used and for this I used Varathane - Professional Clear Finish (Oil Int. Clear Gloss) (946ml). The gloss is great for table tops because it really gives it that bit of shine you notice in a room. For this project I will put three coats on the wood letting it dry about 6 hours in-between coats. You'll use quite a bit more of the varnish than you will the stain, so you want to pickup a larger can - I used the 946ml size (roughly a liter).

[][] The varnish used - note the "Clear Gloss" - this is shiny

Varnishing Tips: (a) give the previous coat time to dry, I gave it at least 6 hours. I didn't put new varnish on if the surface was still tacky (b) making sure you paint it all on evenly. This is a challenge with glossy varnish since it hard to tell where you last painted and (c) making sure that you do a light sanding or use a scrub pad on the previous coating of varnish . This takes away the air bubbles and makes the next coat smoother. In my case three coats was enough.

One warning, during all of this, water is your enemy. You have to use varsol to clean the brushes (not water). You have to make sure the raw wood does NOT come in contact with water or it will stain. I accidentally rested one of the legs in water (just after sanding) and here's what that did to the leg. Notice how prominent the stain is:

[![image][5]][]

Now, the project is finished, the varnish on the table has hardened and I have to say - I'm very happy with it. This is a definite improvement over my previous table sanding project.
