Personal Project: Re-Finishing a Side Table

Fri 21 01 2011

[![image][]][]

As you probably know, I'm a sucker for a new project or something cheap and cool. This time around I needed to find an end table to sit on the left of the couch in the living room. I was thinking this may hold a lamp or perhaps just hold magazines or a nice looking chess set. You may not know this, but Side Tables are damn expensive too. looking at these things I was thinking (1) I don't want to pay a lot for the table and (2) I want it to match the American Walnut stain of other tables in the house - mainly because I suck at matching colors. With that, I went out not specifically looking for a table, but keeping this in mind if I came across it. And, boy, did I get what I was looking for!

[![image][1]][1]

On one fine October day I happened upon the Courtice Flea Market in a small town just to the east of Oshawa. I had never been here previously, but if I were to try and prepare you for this place - I would say it's one big junk pile (I say that as affectionately as I possibly can). I wasn't actually here to look for a table specifically - but I was walking through an area with some interesting furniture piled up and came across a small sturdy looking side table. The table certainly didn't inspire much interest when I first saw it. The table had lots of scratches and paint cracks. But, looking at it from afar, it was the size I was looking for. I decided to get a closer look.

The table when I brought it home

As you see in the above picture, those cracks and scratches were quite plentiful. When I actually went up to the table and picked it up - that's when I decided I wanted it. The weight of the table was quite nice, and, I'm not a person who is able to determined the type of wood by picking it up - but this wood really looked like something I could work with. I decided to talk to the lady about buying the table. Sticker Price: $15 - Price Paid: $10.

The various parts of the table

I was pretty excited when I brought the table back - so much so that I started to work on it right away.

#1 - Sand with 90 Grit sandpaper
After taking the table apart I used an orbital sander with 90 grit sandpaper to get the old stain and finishing off the table. After the first day, the table looked like this:

After the first sanding, I wasn't completely finished

There was something unusual I found after the first day, the wood used on the top level of the table seemed to be different from the wood used on the middle level. Also, it appeared as though the legs were using the same wood as the middle level. The wood on the table top was more of a thin wood covering with possible particle board underneath - not good - I was going to have to take care while sanding this level.

#2 - Sand with 120 grit sandpaper
So, I was being rather touchy with the wood, but I managed to get the first step done without any major damage. Also, learning from the last time I took on a table, I was very careful not to place any of the sanded wood into or around water. This second stage of sanding went quite smooth and quickly. The wood responded nicely to the 120 grit paper as well. Also, I find that I tend to use half as much of the higher grit sandpaper as I do of the lower grit.

Sanding is finished

#3 - Stain the wood

[![image][6]][]

With the wood sanded the way I wanted - I moved on to the staining stage. In this case I decided to use a staining pad for all of this process - including the legs. Given the small surface area that needed stain, I wasn't going to need much for this at all. The stain I used (shown at right) was Varathane, with the "American Walnut" color. I made it a point to keep all of the tables I've stained so far at the same color. I was fond of American Walnut when I first saw it on wood, so I stayed with it. You'll also notice that the can of stain is small - this $8.50 can of stain has so far been enough for me to stain a dining room table, a coffee table and now a side table. Remarkable how much this stain can cover. The key to staining woos seems to be the ability to cover the surface in as uniform a way as you can possibly do. The rest, will be dictated by how well you have sanded and the kind of wood you have. I feel the better the wood, the better absorbed the stain is. This is generally the most important stage of the process.

The wood after just being stained. Notice how dull the surface is.

#4 - Adding the Finish
With this step, I also used a Varathane product for the finishing. For all of my tables, I preferred to use a glossy finish, but you may decide that gloss is a little too shiny. I have been pleased with the Varathane product, so I'll keep using it. For this table, I decided early on that three coats was going to be enough - if the table looked like it was going to need more, I would add more. It turns out three coats was more than enough. It's a good plan to (if you're not sure) stat with three coats and add as you need too. In between coats you want to use a pad on the wood surface to pop any bubbles and generally let the wood dry for at leas 6-10 hours. The longest I left the wood before another coat was a day.

After the first coat of stain

#5 - The final product
The the stain done, and the table dry enough, I put it together. you are really going to want to leave a good long time (maybe a week?) for the finish to harden before actually using the wood. So, here are before and after shots of the table's ultimate location:

Before adding the table

After adding the table
